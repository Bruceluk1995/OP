# Push Retention Chain

Use this for every anime-recap/push-narration draft, regardless of genre or viewpoint. It judges retention structure. Read `push-prompt-architecture.md` first and `push-entertainment-gate.md` with it. Subtitle shape, short lines, pronoun correctness, and connector density do not satisfy these gates.

## Failure Definition

A draft is **not** push narration when it gives one strong opening line and then falls into biography, world explanation, routine training, neutral chronology, or repeated demonstrations of the same conclusion. Breaking that material into short lines creates a short-line synopsis, not an oral push script.

It also fails when it compresses every source event evenly, or when many facts change but those changes form a technical flowchart with no human heat. Push narration is a selected chain of high-value human collisions, not a shorter copy of the full story or a narrated operating procedure.

## Global Production Prompt Contract

Run these stages in order. Do not ask one undifferentiated prompt to invent the story, summarize it, make it oral, add hooks, and self-approve in one pass.

### Stage A — Lock the Delivery

Record only:

```text
lane and audience:
source mode: original premise / source adaptation
presentation: push
viewpoint: first / third
delivery: closed one-shot / serialized cliff
target length or natural length:
reader promise:
dominant emotion:
facts that may not change:
```

Do not reopen these fields during drafting.

### Stage B — Scan for High-Value Events

Before outlining prose, list candidate events and label each one:

```text
event:
visible before -> after:
human effect:
emotional job: emotion entering -> pressure/evidence -> protagonist response -> emotion leaving -> next desire
who wants what / who blocks it:
visible collision and human price:
memorable action or decisive exact line:
function: conflict / reversal / information bomb / relationship turn / visual proof / status-reward / costly choice / payoff
depth: expand / one-line bridge / cut
new audience question:
```

Expand events that change what the audience knows, fears, wants, judges, or expects through a person under pressure. Every expanded event needs an emotional job from the locked audience contract. A changed rule, route, system, contract, or infrastructure state without a choice, loss, refusal, exposure, status change, relationship consequence, or emotional change is bridge material. Bridge routine travel, setup, repeated practice, and necessary chronology in one line. Cut scenery, complete lore, secondary chatter, repeated proof, and any passage whose removal leaves the causal and emotional chain intact.

Rotate event functions. Three larger displays of the same power are one conclusion, not three high points.

### Stage C — Build the Event Chain

Group the selected events into 6-10 flexible major modules. A long one-shot normally needs multiple event beats inside each module; a module is not permission to stretch one action.

For every beat, record:

```text
entering fact/pressure -> choice or collision -> visible result -> changed fact -> next live question
```

A technical state change alone does not qualify. The choice or collision must alter a person's danger, desire, status, relationship, loyalty, or available action.
Also record `emotion entering -> concrete pressure/evidence -> protagonist response -> emotion leaving -> next desire`. Repeating one emotional note at a larger scale creates numbness, not escalation.

Use a one-line bridge only when causality would otherwise break. Do not place three bridge/explanation beats in a row. After every payoff, open a different question rather than returning to setup.

### Stage D — Draft the Spoken Account

- Start from the strongest honest result, contradiction, danger, humiliation, or impossible image.
- Backfill only the minimum cause needed to understand the next decision.
- Expand high-value events; compress bridges; omit cuts.
- Draft natural spoken sentences and short oral paragraphs first. Subtitle splitting happens only after the entertainment and viewpoint gates pass.
- Keep one clear visual action, reveal, reaction, judgment, or causal turn per spoken unit.
- Use concrete verbs and ordinary words. Let the narrator add selection, judgment, or information value; do not merely describe what the picture already shows.
- Convert routine dialogue to reported narration. Preserve exact wording only when it changes status, allegiance, humiliation, choice, or payoff.
- Keep a few decisive human voices in long conflict-driven work. Do not paraphrase every confrontation into the narrator's neutral summary.
- Vary sentence length with function. Conflict may hit in short bursts; a bridge may use one longer sentence.
- Do not add generic audience bait, forced slang, BGM directions, or fake questions unless the requested channel voice uses them.
- Hide the production process. Never let the narrator say `这里不展开 / 为了节省时间 / 后面再说 / 不再赘述 / 同样的过程省略 / 不把三次训练都讲一遍` or equivalents. Perform the compression in the edit; bridge directly to the next changed result.

### Stage E — Oral Rewrite

Read as speech and delete:

- literary scene-setting and essay transitions;
- neutral chronology such as `then / after that / meanwhile` when no causal turn follows;
- explanations the next action already proves;
- repeated subject names, repeated reactions, and repeated skill descriptions;
- technical-noun runs, procedure lists, policy summaries, and companion-as-function choreography;
- connectors or line breaks added only to satisfy a visible ratio;
- abstract emotion that has no action, object, voice, price, wound, status, or consequence;
- any paragraph that exists only to reach a length target.
- editor-facing omission notes or prompt leakage that explain why material was compressed, reordered, or cut.

Preserve clarity. Do not replace understandable narration with a pile of slogan fragments.

### Stage F — Blind Editorial Gate

Read the body without the outline and answer:

```text
first line where I would swipe away:
what I was waiting for:
what the draft gave me instead:
first low-value event expanded too far:
first high-value event compressed too much:
first repeated high-point function:
longest neutral-chronology or explanation run:
first place entertainment dies:
first flowchart/procedure run:
first module without human collision:
title/main promise payoff position:
post-final-payoff share:
five memorable lines, choices, or actions after the opening:
audio-only verdict:
ending branch honored: closed / serialized
verdict and earliest stage to redo:
```

Never let the writer grade its own intentions. Use exact saved-body anchors.

## Build the Chain Before Drafting

Lock a compact `1 + 3 + N` chain:

- **1 stop hook**: the abnormal result, contradiction, humiliation, danger, or impossible status that stops the scroll.
- **3 wall hooks**: three different honest pressures in the opening block. Use a causal gap, immediate consequence, hostile interpretation, deadline, relationship fracture, or dangerous choice. Do not restate the stop hook three times.
- **N continuing pressures**: after the opening, every major module closes on a changed fact plus an unresolved consequence, choice, or question.

This is a retention diagnostic, not a fixed three-line formula. A hook only counts when the audience's understanding, expectation, risk, or desired answer changes.

Before prose, record:

```text
stop hook:
wall hook 1 / new pressure:
wall hook 2 / new pressure:
wall hook 3 / new pressure:
first concrete proof:
main irreversible conflict:
mechanism-changing turn 1:
mechanism-changing turn 2:
earlier debt used by climax:
ending proof action:
high-value event path:
one-line bridges:
cut material:
```

If these fields are merely labels for the same skill proof, crowd reaction, or larger number, rebuild the structure.

## Distinguish Pressure From Setup

- Backstory survives only when it sharpens the current threat or changes the audience's interpretation of the hook.
- Do not answer the opening gap with a biography dump. Reveal the smallest cause needed, then create a new consequence.
- A power reveal is proof, not automatically a reversal.
- A true reversal changes at least one of: goal, threat, rule, tactic, relationship, status, meaning, available choice, or consequence.
- Another student, test, witness, enemy, skill name, rank, or larger spectacle does not count when it proves the same conclusion again.
- A high point must differ in function, not only size. Rotate conflict, revelation, relationship, status, tactic, choice, cost, and payoff.
- A state change does not count as a high point when only pipes, routes, rules, contracts, ledgers, or status panels change and no person's choice, risk, status, or relationship changes with them.
- `but he did not know`, delayed information, and narrator promises do not count without a changed fact in the body.
- A closed one-shot ends after payoff and observable aftermath. Do not import the serialized convention of withholding the ending or promising the next episode.
- A serialized cliff must introduce a concrete new fact, action, arrival, threat, or decision. Hiding an answer the narrator already knows is not a hook.

## Position Diagnostics

Measure positions against non-whitespace body characters after drafting. These are default diagnostics, not platform laws:

- The stop hook begins immediately; do not precede it with atmosphere or routine entry.
- The three wall hooks belong in the opening block, normally within the first 8-12%.
- Deliver the first concrete proof by roughly 10-15%.
- Trigger the main irreversible conflict by roughly 25-30%.
- Change the story mechanism once before the midpoint and again before the final quarter when the length supports it.
- Keep pure setup/backstory below roughly 20% of the body and never place a long uninterrupted setup run directly after the stop hook.
- Pay the title and central promise normally inside the final 15-25% of a closed one-shot. If it pays earlier, name the stronger later promise that remains alive.
- After the final central payoff, keep the default post-final-payoff share below roughly 10-15%: one consequence beat and one ending proof, not a second climax, new mechanism, policy list, or extended procedure.

Missing a diagnostic does not automatically fail an unusual story, but `ready for user read` then requires a concrete explanation of what live pressure performs the same retention function. Length alone is never that explanation.

## Micro-Compression Gate

Use this gate for teaching, training, crafting, diagnosis, investigation, system explanation, and any profession-led scene. These premises often look active while replaying one micro-action for too long.

- Treat professional knowledge as a sharp filter, not as a complete lesson. Keep the one diagnosis that changes the attempt; cut the rest of the curriculum.
- Build the beat as `mistake or obstacle -> decisive correction/choice -> visible abnormal proof -> external consequence/new danger`.
- After the decisive correction, normally show visible proof within the next 2-4 spoken lines. This is a latency diagnostic, not a fixed hook quota. Any extra line must add a new threat, choice, contradiction, or irreversible fact.
- If six or more consecutive spoken lines remain on the same micro-action, question-and-answer drill, procedure, or explanation without a material state change, presume scene failure. Release requires an exact reason each intervening line is necessary.
- In narrative push, explanation may not occupy more space than the proof and its immediate consequence combined. A tutorial request is the exception; a teacher/crafter protagonist is not.
- Show at most one flagship learning process for a repeated upgrade engine. Later upgrades start closer to the changed result and must create a different problem, tactic, cost, relationship, or consequence.
- Do not spend extra process detail to reach a character target. Add an obstacle, choice, opponent adaptation, consequence, or aftermath instead.

The viewer must understand why the action matters, not be able to reproduce the lesson.

## Saved-Body Evidence Map

After drafting, fill this from the saved body, not from the outline or intention. Use a short exact anchor and its character percentage.

```text
total non-whitespace characters:
stop hook: "anchor" @ __%
wall hook 1: "anchor" @ __% -> what new pressure appears
wall hook 2: "anchor" @ __% -> what new pressure appears
wall hook 3: "anchor" @ __% -> what new pressure appears
first concrete proof: "anchor" @ __% -> before / event / after
main irreversible conflict: "anchor" @ __% -> what can no longer reset
mechanism-changing turn 1: "anchor" @ __% -> old problem / new problem
mechanism-changing turn 2: "anchor" @ __% -> old tactic / new tactic
climax debt: "anchor" @ __% -> earlier setup reused
ending proof action: "anchor" @ __%
first repeated conclusion or none:
first repeated high-point function or none:
first low-value event expanded too far or none:
first high-value event compressed too much or none:
longest setup/backstory run:
longest explanation-only or same-micro-action run:
longest neutral-chronology/bridge run:
first flowchart/procedure run:
first module without human collision:
first important character used only as a function/module:
correction/decision -> visible proof latency:
proof -> external consequence latency:
flagship process repeated later or none:
ending branch honored: closed / serialized
title/main promise payoff position:
stronger promise after it or none:
post-final-payoff share:
five memorable lines, choices, or actions after the opening:
audio-only verdict:
```

An empty, vague, outline-only, or repeated-function map blocks release. If the first proof or conflict arrives late, a teaching beat stalls on procedure, or a supposed turn changes only scale, return to structure rather than adding more hook wording.

## Release Order

1. Blind-read the saved body without metrics.
2. Complete the entertainment gate's adversarial release read.
3. Complete the saved-body evidence map.
4. Fix the earliest failed entertainment or retention function.
5. Clear the matching first- or third-person release gate.
6. Split natural oral sentences for subtitles and run surface lint last.

Surface lint may reject oral shape. It can never approve retention or delivery.
