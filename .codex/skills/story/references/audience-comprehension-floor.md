# Audience Comprehension and Curiosity Floor

Apply to audience-facing stories, narration, explainers, titles, outlines, rewrites, and reviews. Clarity creates orientation; it must not explain away curiosity.

## Plain-Core Gate

State the core without special nouns:

`a person does or loses something ordinary -> something visibly changes -> another person is affected`

If the premise cannot survive without ranks, glowing materials, invented institutions, bloodlines, or jargon, rebuild the story engine. A template or definition cannot rescue it.

## Opening

- Give a familiar action, want, loss, choice, conflict, object, bill, wound, or message before lore.
- Allow at most one unexplained setting term before the first visible turn; make it act quickly.
- Introduce institutions through what they do: burn, refuse, seize, accuse, charge, evict, promote, reward, or lose.
- Give a number immediate human meaning: someone cannot pay, loses time, risks status, misses food, or must choose.
- Show what changes before naming a magical material, tier, formula, or legendary quality.
- Do not list preparations because a template asks for them.

## Clarity Stop Rule

Once the audience can answer **who is affected, what changed, and why it matters**, stop explaining and return to action or choice.

- Preserve at least one active unanswered question in each major module.
- Delay rules and history that do not change the current decision.
- Do not translate every causal step after the audience can infer it.
- Do not restate a visible result as a rule, then restate the rule as an emotion.
- Confusion is fixed by a better action or consequence, not by a glossary paragraph.

## Genre Translation

- Male fantasy: prove power through an opponent, object, count, price, route, wound, drop, tactic, or consequence before tier names.
- Female fantasy/romance: prove pressure through accusation, evidence, money, broken promise, public choice, relationship action, or status loss.
- General fiction: establish who wants what and who blocks it before backstory.
- Silver/family drama: use a key, transfer, bill, empty seat, changed lock, unanswered call, meal, signature, or refusal before family history.
- Explainers: begin with a recognizable question, bill, queue, wage, price, rule, or daily decision; translate technical terms into who pays, gains, waits, or changes.

## Early Test

After the first five lines or roughly 150 Chinese/Japanese characters, a casual viewer should answer:

1. Who is doing or suffering something?
2. What changed or is about to change?
3. Why should an ordinary person care?
4. What do I still want to know?

If 1-3 require a glossary, rebuild the event. If 4 has no answer because everything has been explained, restore pressure or uncertainty.

Run the noun-removal test: replace special names, ranks, materials, institutions, and systems with blanks. If no human conflict or visible event remains, the passage is setting delivery, not story.

## Review Rule

Flag comprehension failure as structural. Replace the abstract engine with action, visible change, evidence, choice, or human consequence. Then apply the clarity stop rule; do not reward completeness for its own sake.
