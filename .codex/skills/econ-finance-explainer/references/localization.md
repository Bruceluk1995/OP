# Localization

## Shared Principles

- Keep operator-facing planning notes in Chinese unless the user asks otherwise.
- Separate target language from target audience.
- Localize institutions before examples: mortgages, pensions, student loans, central banks, household debt, labor contracts, taxes, and welfare differ by audience.
- Use target-audience anxieties as the first bridge, not Chinese internet assumptions.
- For Japanese-audience title batches, show Chinese working title plus Japanese publish title by default.

## Japanese Mode

Use natural `です/ます` narration with short lines and clear pivots:

- `ここで重要なのは`
- `では、お金はどこへ流れたのか`
- `問題は、誰がリスクを持っているかです`
- `つまり`
- `ここが少し怖いところです`

Good Japanese title shapes:

- `なぜ普通の人ほど、お金が残りにくいのか`
- `これは節約の問題ではなく、仕組みの問題です`
- `誰が利益を取り、誰がリスクを背負うのか`
- `Xはなぜ「得」に見えて、あとで高くつくのか`

Avoid:

- literal Chinese idioms;
- shouting-style moral judgment;
- China-only housing, exam, or local-debt assumptions unless the topic is China;
- excessive kanbun style.

## English Mode

Use direct, conversational analytical prose:

- `Here is the trap.`
- `The question is not whether people are careless. It is who designed the risk.`
- `Follow the cash flow.`
- `The upside is private, but the downside is distributed.`

Good English title shapes:

- `Why X Looks Cheap But Becomes Expensive`
- `Who Really Pays for X?`
- `The Hidden Risk Behind X`
- `This Is Not Free Money. It Is Risk Transfer.`

## Chinese Mode

Use concise Chinese with strong mechanism language, but do not become a slogan machine.

Good title shapes:

- `为什么普通人越努力，钱越留不住？`
- `这不是省钱问题，而是谁把成本转嫁给了你`
- `看似免费的东西，最后是谁在付账？`
- `当风险被包装成机会，普通人该看懂什么？`

Avoid:

- all-purpose "资本收割" without a specific mechanism;
- overfitted source-character names;
- making every script sound like the same small-town fable.
