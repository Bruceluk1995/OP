# Fact Checking And Safety

Economics and finance scripts often touch high-stakes decisions. Treat facts and recommendations carefully.

## Must Verify

Verify with current reliable sources before writing when the script uses:

- inflation, unemployment, wage, GDP, CPI, PPI, policy-rate, exchange-rate, yield, or housing-price data;
- stock, fund, ETF, bond, crypto, IPO, default, bankruptcy, or company claims;
- tax, pension, welfare, student loan, mortgage, insurance, or regulatory details;
- claims about a current government policy, central-bank decision, or market event.

## Preferred Sources

- Official statistics and central banks: Statistics Bureau of Japan, Bank of Japan, Cabinet Office Japan, MHLW, MIC, FRED, BLS, BEA, Federal Reserve, ECB, Eurostat, OECD, IMF, World Bank.
- Regulators and exchanges: SEC, FINRA, FSA Japan, ESMA, national securities regulators, stock-exchange filings.
- Company facts: annual reports, investor relations, official filings.
- Market prices: use the finance tool or reputable market data, and state the date/time if relevant.

## Safety Boundaries

- Provide education, not personalized financial advice.
- Do not tell the user or viewer to buy, sell, hold, short, lever, refinance, or allocate to a specific asset.
- If discussing a risky product, explain risk structure, fees, leverage, liquidity, counterparty risk, and who benefits.
- When using examples, distinguish fictional analogies from real market facts.
- For tax, legal, pension, and debt-relief topics, include a concise "rules differ by jurisdiction" note when appropriate.

## Evidence Style

Do not overload the narration with citations. Put source notes before or after the script, while keeping the narration clean.

Inside the narration, use facts lightly:

- one number to make scale visible;
- one comparison to prove the mechanism;
- one current event only if it directly supports the explanation.

If facts are not verified, mark the item as "needs verification" and do not write it as settled truth.
