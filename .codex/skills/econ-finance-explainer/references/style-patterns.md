# Style Patterns

## Corpus-Derived Profile

The source corpus is not a neutral textbook voice. It explains economics through pressure felt by ordinary people: rent, degrees, jobs, debt, platform traffic, AI subscriptions, leverage, and bad decisions that look rational inside a trap.

Reusable rhetorical functions:

- Turn an abstract term into a visible scene before naming it.
- Ask "who gets the upside, who keeps the risk?"
- Show the decision from the ordinary person's point of view, then reveal the system behind it.
- Use a small fictional economy to simplify a large financial mechanism.
- End with a practical rule or warning.

Common source moves:

- `这就是`: lock the mechanism after the story.
- `核心`: identify the decisive variable.
- `本质`: strip away surface morality.
- `所以`: push from story to rule.
- `恰恰`: reverse the obvious interpretation.

Use these functions naturally in the target language. Do not copy exact phrasing.

## Voice

- Concrete, fast, and slightly sharp.
- Sympathetic to ordinary people, but not sentimental.
- Skeptical of "free money", "risk-free yield", "easy success", and "technology will automatically liberate everyone."
- Willing to name incentives: fees, leverage, pricing power, information asymmetry, credit expansion, debt rollover, traffic allocation, labor bargaining.
- Uses analogies like bills, buckets, queues, theater seating, casino tables, toll gates, conveyor belts, and repair shops.

## Opening Formula

Open with one of:

- Ordinary wallet scene: salary arrives, rent leaves, subscription renews, loan payment hits.
- Market lure: "everyone says this is easy money."
- Policy paradox: government spends more, yet ordinary people feel poorer.
- Fictional town story: a mayor, shop owner, worker, landlord, banker, platform, or school.
- Data hook: a verified number that makes the problem urgent.

By the first 8-12 lines, the viewer should know:

- what money problem is being explained;
- why the obvious answer is insufficient;
- who seems to benefit and who seems to pay.

## Argument Engine

1. Scene: a concrete person or institution faces a money choice.
2. Surface explanation: the popular reason people give.
3. Mechanism: price, debt, rate, risk, incentive, regulation, monopoly, information, or bargaining power.
4. Transfer: identify who moves cost or risk onto whom.
5. Feedback loop: show why the behavior repeats or scales.
6. Comparison: one adjacent case, country, platform, cycle, or historical episode.
7. Rule: a short takeaway that can survive beyond the current event.

## What To Avoid

- Pure outrage without mechanism.
- Overusing "capital" as a magic villain word.
- Turning every topic into class struggle when the actual mechanism is tax, duration risk, liquidity, demographics, or regulation.
- Repeating the same fictional city, same casino analogy, same worker-vs-boss setup in every episode.
- Treating current financial claims as stable without checking dates and sources.
