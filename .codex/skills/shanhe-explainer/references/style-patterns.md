# Style Patterns

## Corpus-Derived Profile

The corpus favors analytical short videos over story recaps. Titles and scripts usually ask a concrete question, reject the obvious answer, then rebuild the topic through institutions, incentives, logistics, geography, technology, or human weakness.

Typical Chinese original length is 5,000-9,000 characters, with some long pieces above 12,000. The strongest topics are not "what happened" but "why did this apparently irrational thing make sense under its constraints."

High-frequency rhetorical functions in the Chinese corpus:

- "所以": causal pivot and section stitching.
- "核心": reduce the problem to one decisive lever.
- "这就是": lock a conclusion after evidence.
- "本质": strip away surface morality or narrative.
- "问题是": introduce contradiction or hidden constraint.
- "恰恰": reverse the audience's assumption.

Use these functions in Japanese/English with natural equivalents, not literal repetition.

## Subject Boundary

The corpus is a style source, not a subject plan. Do not reproduce the channel's Chinese-history menu by default. For this project, the default content lanes are:

- world history and foreign history;
- Japan-focused history, society, economy, institutions, and geopolitics for Japanese audiences;
- geopolitics and strategy;
- institutions, money, tax, trade, and bureaucracy;
- science, technology, climate, math, AI, and civilization;
- social common sense explained through incentives.

Chinese dynastic history is allowed only when the user explicitly requests it or when it is one comparative example inside a global topic.

## Title Formula

Good titles combine:

1. A sharp question:
   - Why did X fail despite advantage?
   - How did X survive longer than expected?
   - Why does everyone misunderstand Y?
   - What was the real constraint behind Z?
2. A counterintuitive half-answer:
   - not because they were weak, but because they were trapped by structure.
   - the problem was not courage, but logistics.
   - the visible villain was only the surface.
3. A light punchline or judgment when appropriate:
   - "you are not looking at a battle; you are looking at an accounting problem."
   - "this was not strategy. It was a bill coming due."

Avoid titles that only summarize a topic: "The history of X", "An introduction to Y", "A complete explanation of Z".

## Opening Hook

Open with one of these, never with generic background:

- Scene hook: date, place, person, action, and one strange detail.
- Meme hook: familiar internet phrase or daily-life analogy that reframes the topic.
- Data hook: a surprising number that makes the question urgent.
- Paradox hook: two facts that should not coexist.
- Wrong-answer hook: state the popular explanation, then show why it cannot explain the facts.

The opening must reach the central question quickly. By the first 8-12 lines, the audience should know what mystery they are watching.

## Argument Engine

A strong script moves through layered constraints:

1. Surface event: what people think the story is about.
2. Immediate mechanism: the practical reason something happened.
3. Structural mechanism: institution, geography, technology, finance, logistics, succession, class, or information.
4. Human mechanism: fear, face, greed, loyalty, risk avoidance, family logic, or career incentive.
5. Comparative proof: one adjacent case that shows the mechanism is not accidental.
6. Final thesis: the visible event was a symptom; the hidden structure was the cause.

## Voice

- Assertive but not empty-macho.
- Dry, slightly cynical, often amused by institutional absurdity.
- Comfortable using modern analogies for ancient or technical problems.
- Uses short rhetorical questions to reset attention.
- Treats "morality" as insufficient unless paired with incentives and constraints.

## Humor

Humor should work as compression:

- Turn a complex constraint into a vivid everyday comparison.
- Personify systems sparingly: a dynasty, market, army, or bureaucracy can "panic", "calculate", or "send the bill".
- Use punchlines after the evidence, not before it.
- Avoid random jokes, memes that require Chinese internet context in Japanese/English, or jokes that weaken factual clarity.

## Common Failure Modes

- Chronology dump: dates and names without a central pressure.
- Wikipedia voice: neutral explanation with no thesis.
- Overheated nationalism or moral certainty without evidence.
- Literal translation of Chinese idioms into Japanese/English.
- China-centered examples when the target viewer is Japanese, American, or European and the topic does not require China.
- Excessive "in essence" phrases without actual mechanism.
- Ending with "history teaches us" instead of a concrete conclusion.
