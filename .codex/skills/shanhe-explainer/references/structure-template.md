# Structure Template

Use this for full scripts and outlines.

## Standard Script Arc

For a complete episode, default to around 15,000 Japanese/Chinese characters unless the user explicitly asks for a short version. The script must feel like a long analytical video, not a stretched short clip.

### 1. Cold Open

Length: 8-12% of script.

Start inside a vivid moment:

- date/place/action for history;
- a current dilemma for strategy or finance;
- a number or everyday observation for science/social topics;
- a funny but relevant analogy for high-click openings.

End the cold open with the central question.

### 2. Audience Lock

Length: 8-10% of script.

Make the first comparison, analogy, or anxiety native to the target audience:

- Japan: resource dependence, aging society, bureaucracy, islands/sea lanes, Meiji/Tokugawa memory, company or school hierarchy.
- United States: federalism, constitutional fights, markets, military reach, tax bills, personal freedom versus institutions.
- Europe: nation-state versus EU, welfare, energy, colonial afterlives, war memory, borders, bureaucracy, coalition politics.

This does not mean flattering the audience. It means the opening should feel like the problem is theirs, not a translated Chinese talking point.

### 3. Question Lock

Length: 8-10% of script.

State what the audience probably thinks, then narrow it:

- "Most people think this was about X. That explains part of it, but not the key."
- "If the answer were only X, then Y would not have happened."
- "So the real question is not A. It is B."

### 4. First Mechanism

Length: 18-22% of script.

Explain the most concrete layer first: money, troops, geography, supply, law, information, incentives, technology, or succession.

Use examples before abstraction. Do not give a big concept until the audience has seen the problem operate.

### 5. Second Mechanism

Length: 18-22% of script.

Raise the level:

- institution behind the event;
- actor incentives;
- hidden dependency;
- structural contradiction;
- constraint that makes the "obvious solution" impossible.

### 6. Comparative Turn

Length: 12-16% of script.

Bring in one comparison:

- same person in another period;
- neighboring country/dynasty/company;
- similar technology or institution;
- failed alternative path.

The comparison must prove the mechanism, not decorate the script.

### 7. Reversal

Length: 10-12% of script.

Use the evidence to invert the title question:

- X did not fail because it was stupid; X failed because its strength created the trap.
- Y did not survive by luck; Y survived because nobody could afford the alternative.
- Z was not a moral accident; it was the system behaving normally.

### 8. Final Judgment

Length: 6-8% of script.

End with one clean thesis and one memorable image. Do not end with vague preaching.

Good ending functions:

- "This is the bill that finally came due."
- "The real battlefield was not the battlefield."
- "The tragedy was not that they made no choice; it was that every available choice had already been poisoned."

## 15,000-Character Rhythm

Use this when drafting a complete episode.

The goal is not more words. The goal is more turns. A 15,000-character Shanhe-style script should normally contain 14-18 meaningful beats, not one 8-beat outline stretched thin.

### Beat Density

Add a new pressure, proof, or reversal every 700-1,000 Japanese/Chinese characters:

- a sharper version of the question;
- a concrete scene that shows the mechanism operating;
- a number, date, map, policy, institution, or technical constraint;
- a human incentive: fear, face, career risk, family burden, risk avoidance;
- a cost-transfer point: who pays, who benefits, who cannot exit;
- a comparison that proves the mechanism is not accidental;
- a consequence that changes the viewer's understanding.

If a 1,000-character stretch only repeats the same emotion, cut or redesign it.

### Long-Form Expansion Ladder

When the first draft is under 15,000 characters, expand in this order:

1. Add a second concrete scene, preferably from another social layer.
2. Add one missing mechanism: finance, logistics, law, bureaucracy, labor, technology, geography, or information.
3. Add one failed obvious solution and explain why it fails.
4. Add one comparison that proves the mechanism.
5. Add one human incentive layer, but keep it tied to the structure.
6. Add one consequence for ordinary people or institutions.
7. Add a final callback image that answers the opening.

Do not expand by adding weather, room atmosphere, repeated adjectives, vague lament, or moral slogans.

### Pacing Shape

For a 15,000-character complete episode, aim for this rhythm:

- 0-1,500 chars: hook, scene, contradiction, central question.
- 1,500-3,000 chars: audience-native anxiety and wrong-answer rejection.
- 3,000-6,000 chars: first mechanism, with concrete examples.
- 6,000-9,000 chars: second mechanism, higher-level structure, and actor incentives.
- 9,000-11,500 chars: comparison or counterfactual, proving the mechanism.
- 11,500-13,500 chars: reversal, cost transfer, and consequences.
- 13,500-15,500 chars: final judgment, callback image, and one clean thesis.

This is a rhythm guide, not a hard chapter divider. Do not put visible headings inside the narration unless the user asks for a production script.

### Anti-Water Gate

Before delivery, check:

- Can every 700-1,000 characters be summarized as a new beat?
- Does every analogy clarify a mechanism?
- Does every statistic or source change the conclusion?
- Does every scene put pressure on the central question?
- If one paragraph is removed, does the argument get weaker?

If the answer is no, revise instead of keeping the paragraph for length.

## New User Intake

When simulating or guiding a new user, ask in this order:

1. Target audience: Japan, United States, Europe, or Other/international.
2. User state, phrased as "what do you have now?":
   - no topic yet, needs ideas;
   - has keyword/hot event;
   - has title/topic, needs outline;
   - ready to write full script;
   - has video/subtitles to analyze.
3. If the user has no topic yet, ask topic source:
   - live trend scouting;
   - evergreen topic design;
   - user-provided topic seeds.
4. Topic lane:
   - If target audience is Japan, show Japan-focused history/society as the first option.
   - Core lanes: Japan-focused history/society, world history, geopolitics/strategy, institutions/economy, science/technology, or social common sense.
5. Target language and length.
6. Specific topic or let the agent propose topics.

Do not ask for topic lane before the target audience unless the audience is already explicit.
Do not generate a title batch before deciding whether the source is trend-driven or evergreen.

For Japan-audience simulations, use this topic menu:

1. 日本本土史 / 日本社会 / 日本制度
2. 世界史 / 外国史
3. 国际战略 / 地缘政治
4. 制度经济 / 权力机制
5. 科学技术 / 文明演化
6. 社会常识 / 人间清醒

For Japan-audience title batches, use this source menu before the topic menu:

1. Google Trends JP 热点选题
2. 常青选题，不追热点
3. 我给关键词，你帮我转成讲解标题

For Japan-audience title display, default to bilingual output:

1. 中日双语标题（默认，推荐）
2. 只看中文方向，之后再翻日语
3. 只要日语标题

If the user chooses Japanese title generation but the operator language is Chinese, still include a Chinese working title or Chinese explanation beside each Japanese title unless the user explicitly asks for Japanese-only.

For new-user simulations, use this user-state menu after audience selection:

```text
你现在是哪种情况？

1. 我还没有题目，想先找能做视频的选题
2. 我有一个关键词/热点，想包装成标题
3. 我已有题目，想先做讲解稿大纲
4. 我已有题目或大纲，想直接写完整稿
5. 我有视频/字幕，想拆解套路
```

Avoid saying only "生成选题/标题、做大纲、写正文" without explanation. Those are internal workflow labels, not clear onboarding copy.

When asking Google Trends category scope, say clearly: this only decides what to fetch, not what to output. The previously selected topic lane remains mandatory.

For Japan-audience trend category scope:

1. 只抓强相关分类
2. 全分类抓取，但最终仍严格过滤到已选题材
3. Agent decides by selected topic lane

If the user selected `日本本土史 / 日本社会 / 日本制度`, do not output overseas sports, foreign celebrity, match schedule, or transfer-news titles. If the trend feed is mostly those, say the trend feed is weak for the selected lane and ask whether to broaden or mix evergreen topics.

## Full Script Checks

Before delivery:

- The first 12 lines contain a scene, paradox, or number, not generic background.
- Every 700-1,000 Japanese/Chinese characters or 350-500 English words introduces a new piece of evidence, mechanism, consequence, or sharper inference.
- At least one common explanation is rejected or refined.
- At least one analogy makes an abstract mechanism concrete.
- The conclusion answers the title directly.
- If current facts are used, the fact-check notes are available in the conversation or source list.
- A complete Japanese or Chinese episode should normally reach at least 15,000 characters. If it is shorter, add real explanatory state changes: another mechanism layer, a comparison, an evidence turn, a concrete scene, a counterargument, or a consequence. Do not stretch with scenery or adjectives.
