## Benchmark Protocol

Use hot works only as structural references: interspecies rule, political marriage, feared spouse, culture misunderstanding, nonhuman vulnerability, and acceptance scene. If available, read `对标/monster-demon-interspecies-romance/{work}/拆文报告.md`.

## Core Engine

- The couple is separated by species, enemy status, myth, curse, lifespan, food/courtship custom, magic, or political duty.
- The heroine's wound may be human society's rejection, a political offering, saint/hero duty, or a false rumor about monsters.
- The male lead is powerful but socially misunderstood; his tenderness must appear through nonhuman-specific actions, not generic devotion.
- Romance turns on learning rules: name, touch, food, oath, territory, scent, scale/horn/wings, magic contract, or clan custom.
- The payoff is public or intimate acceptance across a cultural boundary.

## Six-Episode Season

1. Political or forced encounter: heroine meets the feared nonhuman spouse/enemy under pressure.
2. First rule learned: a custom misunderstanding becomes a tenderness or danger beat.
3. Human-side threat: former allies, church, hero party, or court uses prejudice against the couple.
4. Nonhuman vulnerability: heroine protects or understands the male lead's hidden rule.
5. Boundary test: oath, ceremony, battle, clan law, or diplomatic venue forces a public choice.
6. Chosen bond: species prejudice loses authority and the couple sets a new rule or alliance.

## Required Payoffs

- Interspecies rule.
- Culture misunderstanding.
- Specific nonhuman tenderness.
- Human/social threat.
- Public or ritual choice.
- Acceptance with a next-world hook.

## Avoid

- Making the monster/demon/dragon only a normal duke with horns.
- Romance based only on kidnapping or ownership unless the user explicitly wants dark tones.
- Ignoring political consequences of enemy/species marriage.
